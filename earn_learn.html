Learn & Earn

A skill-sharing, peer-tutoring platform
that connects students based on individual’s learning need

Type Individual Project
Role UX/UI Designer
Duration Fall 2018 (currently in process) | SI 582: Intro to Interaction Design
Service Slogan Share your skills, Make some money, and Go Blue!

Overview

The University of Michigan (UM) is a reputable, well-rounded school with over 50,000 students and faculties. All graduate programs including engineering, law, business, information, public policy, social work and more, are mostly ranked top 10 in the states.

One day, when I had hard time figuring out how to solve issues in carrying out my website coding, I thought, ‘Since we have so many intelligent students from such a wide range of disciplines, what if I could have a peer tutor from someone who can help me out on the specific problems that I’m having?’, ‘what if UM students could exchange or teach each other's knowledge and skills according to their learning needs?’

I decided to take this questions for my individual project in class SI 582: Introduction to Interaction Design, taught by Professor McCarley. In fact, I realized that many UM students have been raising the similar questions when they fail to get help from school resources or finding a right person who can work on their problems together.

Based on tailored research, I finally created “Earn & Learn” , an application that connects UM students based on individual’s needs for learning. Learning subjects could be anything from courseworks to technical skills, and students can be both a tutor and tutee. The application was presented at the final presentation during the class, and received an “A” in overall evaluation.

Goals

Earn & Learn allows users to get connected among UM students in order to:


1) exchange each other’s needed knowledge for free
2) find a credible peer tutor nearby at affordable price
3) become a tutor to gain teaching experiences and make money

Approach

I followed the user-centered approach as below:



When I approached the problem, Professor Mccarley highly emphasized the following:

“ ... I don’t care about how fancy your design would be. UX design is NOT graphic design anymore. In your field of work, there is a huge possibility that you will be given existing design guidelines. So, again, UX Design is not about artwork, but all about problem-solving. UX Designer always should think 'am I really solving my users’ problems?'..."

That was such a powerful advice for me, so tried my very best to keep her words in mind whenever I approached each stages of problem solving.

Framing the problem

In this stage, I conducted user research and competitive analysis to frame the problem.

For user research, I utilized methods of survey, contextual inquiry, and user interviews.

For competitive analysis, I took a critical look at existing solutions within my context and analyzed what their pros and cons are in respect to supporting my target activity. I particularly selected four key players in the industry, and two existing tutoring system from the UM. By doing this, I was able to compile a list of problems or frustrating aspects of the current solutions.

Key Findings & Problem Statement



Combining all data from user research and competitive analysis, I organized key findings and finally, wrote a problem statement.
