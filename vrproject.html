King of Speech VR Design

How Might We Help Users to overcome
a fear of public speaking or to practice public speaking

Type Team Project | Ruiting Cao, Cindy Zhu, Shih-ting Huang, Valorie Zhang, Yuehan Wang, April Shin
Role UX Designer
Tools A-Frame, Unity
Timespan Fall 2019 | 2 weeks

Overview

Over the last four months, I was taking the course: introduction to AR/VR, taught by Professor Nebeling. For the first two weeks, everyone in the class had an opportunity to present their own ideas, a.k.a. speed dating, and could form a group based on individual's interest. I formed a group with five amazing classmates and started working on defining a problem space that we would like to solve through VR.

The Problem Statement & Key Features

After a few hours of heated discussion, we finalized our problem statement: how might we help users to overcome a fear of public speaking or to practice public speaking freely until they become confident. I found this problem is very interesting and also prospective because 1) from the user perspective, the needs for public speaking has increased over the last decades, and 2) from the business perspective, the solution can be marketable for both students and adults.

The product’s key features is to provide 1) a real public speaking setting depending on users’ needs, 2) real audience experience by incorporating sounds or visual components, and 3) unexpected situations in order for users to practice/handle difficulties during public speaking.

Physical Prototype

To begin with, we started out with physical prototyping. There were two major interactions: 1) our core user is waiting at the backstage right before starting his/her speech. As soon as the user taps on “start the speech,” the curtain will open and the user can see the audiences. And 2) during or after the user's public speaking, unexpected or pre-made questions from the audience will show up on the question box so that the user can simulate some difficult situation and think about how to overcome it.

Digital Prototype

After the physical prototyping, we were able to receive diverse feedback from the class presentation. First, from Professor Nebeling, we received feedback to focus more on creating immersive experience by incorporating 1) sound, 2) lighting, and 3) surrounded background. Second, from peers, we had feedback to add more realistic audiences who are moving like a real human.

In order to incorporate the feedback, we added visual and audio elements to the final prototyping, and also explored many different ways to add live 3D models that we could use as an audience, which we ended up implementing it successfully. Specifically, to best mimic the real speech environment, we adjusted the camera positions to make sure the user “stands” at the center of the stage, close to the back of the curtain. In addition, we adjusted question plate height to make sure it’s in a comfortable headturn range when the user clicks to pick up the question.

Key Learnings

There are several lessons I learned throughout the project.

First, VR requires many more elements to take into consideration.
Unlike mobile/web design, VR should give users a high-dimensional immersive experience, which I had to take a lot of sensory and physical components into the actual prototyping. This had me to think that VR experience design is what I should pay more attention and put more effort/time in building compared to that of mobile/web experience.

Second, learning effective prototyping tools is imperative.
We, as a team, gave several shots to learn numerous prototyping tools, and ended up using A-Frame since most of us are familiar with HTML. However, VR prototyping is still much more complicated than the other types of prototyping. Even though A-Frame is HTML based, we need to think in 3D while manipulating the elements. For example, when we tried to make the curtain being disappeared by clicking, it took us a lot of time to figure out how we should change its visibility and also its position to fully move it outside of the sight - it was very interesting to think of the coding in 3D space. Overall, compared to any other design, I felt learning effective tools seems very important to fully learn designing VR experience.

Experience & Skills

Throughout the project and overall coursework, I gained new experience and learned new skill sets.

First and foremost, I learned how to build VR product by using diverse methods and tools.
For the product I built with the team, I used A-frame, but not only the A-frame, I was able to try out another new tool, Unity. While spending several hours and days to learn new tools, I could gain the ability to design 3/4D space as well as gain different perspectives for UX in larger context.

Second, I had an opportunity to learn the imperative principles in designing VR experience.
Through numerous readings, lectures, and class activities, I learned how VR technology can be translated into a new user experience. This further convinced me that keeping up with new technology and learning new design principles, tools and even ethics are very important as a UX designer.
